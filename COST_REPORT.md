## At-scale estimate (10,000+ raw podcast hours)

This trial moved 179.09 raw hours through 6 RTX 3090 pods for $16.02 ($0.09/raw-hour, $0.16/usable-hour). Scaling 50-100x in raw-hour volume does not scale every cost line the same way -- some are linear, some are sublinear, and the real bottleneck shifts.

**Compute (linear, the dominant cost line at any scale).** `gpu_compute` is 97% of this trial's spend and stays the dominant line at scale -- it's pure wall-clock pod-uptime × hourly rate with no economies of scale on RunPod's Community Cloud pricing. At this trial's measured $0.09/raw-hour, 10,000 raw hours of compute alone projects to roughly **$900**, before accounting for the throughput ceiling below. The real planning number isn't the dollar figure (still cheap relative to most cloud GPU budgets) -- it's **wall-clock**: this trial ran 6 pods in ~8.5h and processed 179 raw hours, i.e. ~3.5 raw-hours/pod-hour aggregate throughput. Hitting 10,000 raw hours in a comparable ~24h window at that same per-pod rate needs roughly `10,000 / (3.5 × 24) ≈ 120` pods running concurrently -- a step change from 6, not a small increment. Diarization (pyannote) is the confirmed bottleneck stage (see WRITEUP.md's bottleneck discussion); at scale this argues for either far more pods, or batching/queueing diarization onto fewer, busier GPUs with a proper job queue (Celery/RQ-style) instead of one Python process per pod owning a static shard.

**Storage (sublinear, stays a rounding error).** This trial's actual measured footprint is 8.87GB for 102.4 usable hours (~87MB/usable-hour, FLAC-encoded speech-only after VAD trimming and clip segmentation -- raw audio is processed-and-deleted per episode, never retained). At 10,000+ raw hours (and a comparable ~57% yield, so ~5,700+ usable hours), that's roughly **~500GB of clip storage** -- at R2's $0.015/GB-month, on the order of **single-digit dollars per month** to retain, not a cost driver. Class A op costs (one PutObject per clip + manifest + db snapshots) scale with clip count, not bytes: this trial's 209K candidate clips cost $0.47 in Class A ops; 50-100x that volume is still only on the order of **tens of dollars**, comfortably inside what a few months of the 1M-op free tier doesn't already cover.

**Network egress: still $0 by design** -- R2 charges no egress fees regardless of scale (Cloudflare's actual differentiator vs. S3 for this exact workload: many small downstream reads of clip audio by training pipelines/researchers cost nothing extra).

**What concretely changes at 10,000+ raw hours (not just "more of the same"):**
- **Podcast discovery and selection** need to move off ad hoc PodcastIndex/iTunes querying into a maintained catalog with dedup logic at the *podcast* level, not just the per-shard episode_id collision handling this trial relied on -- at 50-100x the show count, RSS feed churn (removed episodes, re-hosted audio, feed URL changes) becomes a steady-state operational concern, not a one-off.
- **Job orchestration replaces static sharding.** This trial's `assigned_shard` column (set once before any pod starts) works for ~700 episodes across 6 pods; at 10,000+ hours with 100+ pods it stops being viable -- a dynamic work queue (claim-and-lease semantics, dead-pod episode reclaim) is needed so slow/fast pods self-balance instead of running until a fixed shard drains.
- **Centralized speaker re-clustering becomes the scaling bottleneck of its own kind.** `recluster_podcast_from_scratch` is O(embeddings for that podcast) and runs once per podcast at merge time -- fine for 58 podcasts, but if podcast count also scales 10-20x, either parallelize re-clustering across podcasts (it's already independent per podcast, so this is an easy win) or move to incremental-only matching with periodic (not full-recompute) re-clustering, accepting slightly more clustering drift in exchange for not re-deriving every embedding's assignment from scratch every merge.
- **A dedicated music/ad classifier stops being optional.** `ad_segment_heuristic` and `music_detected` are explicitly the weakest filters in this trial's stack (see LIMITATIONS.md) -- at this trial's scale the absolute clip counts they mis-classify are small enough to eyeball-sanity-check; at 50-100x scale the same heuristics' weaknesses would mis-classify a correspondingly larger absolute volume invisibly. A real spectral/learned classifier (or YAMNet-style off-the-shelf audio event model) is worth the added GPU pass at that point.
- **Tiered output, following Libri-Light's own precedent** (see WRITEUP.md's Prior art section): Libri-Light ships three tiers (unlab-600/6k/60k) rather than one undifferentiated pool. At 10,000+ hours, the same split is worth adopting here -- a "strict" tier (conservative `match_threshold`, high diarization confidence, low discard-heuristic uncertainty) for speaker-ID-sensitive downstream use, and a "broad" tier (current thresholds, higher yield, flagged lower-confidence) for volume-sensitive use -- rather than picking one threshold that's wrong for both use cases.
- **Production metrics/alerts/retries/data-quality checks that this trial only partially needed:** (a) per-pod heartbeat staleness already has a watchdog (`scripts/poll_status.py`, 15-20min threshold) -- at 100+ pods this needs to become a dashboard with per-pod drill-down, not a script a human runs manually; (b) aggregate yield-rate and discard-reason-histogram drift should alert (not just report after the fact) if a podcast's discard rate spikes well outside this trial's observed 57.2% yield / `too_short`-dominated histogram, since that's the cheapest early signal that a podcast's audio doesn't fit the pipeline's assumptions (e.g. music-heavy show, non-English despite metadata, heavy ad-read density); (c) the duration-distribution deviation this trial actually hit (97.6% under-10s vs. a 70% target, see PROCESSING_SUMMARY.md/LIMITATIONS.md) should be a standing dashboard metric with a threshold alert, not something only discovered by manually running `validate_manifest.py`; (d) failed-episode-rate by failure category (this trial: 15/693 failed, all at `downloading`, root-caused to a CDN bot-management block -- see PROBLEMS.md #19) should be tracked per-domain/per-CDN so a new systematically-blocked host is caught in hours, not discovered by manual log review.
